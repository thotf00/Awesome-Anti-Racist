# SceneOnRadio Season 2 Seeing White Podcast Series - Episode 1 : Seeing Whiteness

#### Focus: Structural racism: institutionalized patterns of exploitation and oppression.

### I. Core Premise and Intent of the "Seeing White" Series

- **A Radical Shift in Focus:** The series explicitly aims to "turn the lens around." Instead of the standard journalistic practice of focusing on people of color, it will examine white America and the very concept of "whiteness" as a subject of inquiry.
- **Naming the Unnamed:** Host John Biewen reflects on his own career, acknowledging that while he has told many stories about Black, Latino, and Native American communities, he has typically left white people as a racial group unnamed, treating whiteness like an unexamined "elephant in the room."
- **Investigating Whiteness as a Construct:** The central questions of the series are foundational: Where did the idea of a "white race" originate? Is it a divine or natural creation, or is it a human-made invention? If it was constructed, what was the purpose? How has its meaning shifted over centuries, and how does it function in society today?
- **Examining Cultural Narratives:** The series will probe the stories we collectively carry about whiteness—often unconsciously—and question whether those stories are true.

### II. The Societal Context: The Urgency Behind the Examination

- **The 2016 Election as a Flashpoint:**
    - **D.L. Hughley's Provocative Thesis:** Biewen recounts being stopped in his tracks by a clip from *The View*. Comedian D.L. Hughley stated: "Obama is what we would like to be. Donald Trump and his supporters are what we are." Biewen initially bristled at being included in Hughley's "we," but after Trump's victory, the statement took on new weight.
    - **The "White-lash":** The episode cites the statistic that 70% of voters in 2016 were white, and 58% of them voted for Trump. This contextualizes Van Jones's famous CNN comment that Trump's victory was a "white-lash against a changing country" and "against a Black president."
    - **The Question of Racism's Role:** The transcript acknowledges that while the precise role of "straight-up racism" in Trump's victory is debatable, his campaign's rhetoric of white resentment was not a deal-breaker for over 62 million voters.
- **A Catalog of Racial Flashpoints:** The series is positioned as a response to a relentless stream of recent events that have turned a spotlight onto whiteness:
    - **Violence and Terrorism:** Police shootings of unarmed Black people, and the massacre of nine Black churchgoers by white supremacist Dylann Roof.
    - **Viral Evidence of Everyday Racism:** The episode includes audio of a racist chant from University of Oklahoma fraternity members ("There will never be a nigger SAE... You can hang 'em from a tree...") and a confrontation in Durham, NC, where a white woman furiously harasses a brown-skinned man, calling him a "f*cking Muslim" and a "son of a nigger-loving atheist bitch." The man calmly responds, "I feel sorry for you, that you're full of hatred."
    - **Cultural Movements:** Instances like "Oscars So White," highlighted by Chris Rock's joke calling the awards the "White People's Choice Awards."

### III. Critical Analytical Framework (Chenjerai Kumanyika's Role)

- **Introducing a Critical Voice:** John Biewen brings in Chenjerai Kumanyika, a professor of critical cultural media studies, to act as a co-pilot and "check" his perspective as a white man. Kumanyika humorously clarifies he will not "speak for all people of color," but for his own "smart and thoughtful self."
- **Rejecting the "Race Relations" Frame:** Kumanyika articulates a key concern: the series must avoid the dominant, yet flawed, American framing of racial issues as "race relations."
    - **The Problem with "Race Relations":** This framing over-focuses on individual attitudes and prejudice. It reduces racism to a question of "who has it?"—a puzzle of interpersonal bigotry.
    - **Obama as an Example:** Kumanyika points out that even President Obama has sometimes framed the issue this way, highlighting how pervasive this limited view is.
- **Centering Structural Racism:** The series will instead focus on "structural racism" or "institutionalized patterns of exploitation and oppression."
    - **Racism Without Racists:** The crucial thesis is that racism can exist and thrive without individual "racists." Systems and structures have been set up over time in ways that perpetuate racialized outcomes automatically, often incentivizing people without prejudiced attitudes to participate in an oppressive system.
    - **The Focus on Power:** The conversation must shift from personal niceness ("How are we getting along?") to a complex engagement with power: how it works, who has what rights, and how resources are distributed.
- **Distinguishing from Overt White Supremacy:**
    - **A Key Disclaimer:** Biewen and Kumanyika explicitly state that the series is *not* about neo-Nazis, the KKK, or explicit white nationalists. While those groups are having a moment, the series will focus on the "rest of us."
    - **The Goal: Seeing the Invisible Water:** The real target is the "everyday," institutionalized, and often invisible whiteness that shapes interactions and is "ingrained" in how institutions are set up. It's the water we're all swimming in and thus fail to see.

### IV. Narrative Hooks and Personal Reflections

- **John Biewen's Journey:** The episode is framed by Biewen's personal story. He moves from an initial, defensive reaction to Hughley's "we" ("Wasn't sure I wanted to be implicated in that 'we'") to a recognition of the reality of Trump's win and the validity of the critique. This models the kind of introspection the series hopes to encourage in its listeners.
- **The "White Meeting" Humor:** The episode uses a light-hearted exchange between Stephen Colbert and Jon Batiste about "the white meeting" to humorously acknowledge white social spaces and in-group dynamics, setting the stage for a more serious examination.
- **Chenjerai's "Deputizing" of John:** In a moment of dark humor, Kumanyika "deputizes" Biewen, as a white person, to go out into the world and intervene when he hears people frame racism only in terms of individual prejudice.

### V. A Preview of the Journey Ahead

- **Next Episode Teaser:** The introduction concludes by setting up the next installment, which will travel back in time to an era before the concept of race existed.
- **Historian Nell Irvin Painter's Insight:** A clip from historian Nell Irvin Painter is used to pose the core question: In the past, people saw variations in skin color, but "what did that mean?" The answer, she suggests, is nothing—there was "no notion of race." This sets the stage for exploring the *invention* of the white race.
